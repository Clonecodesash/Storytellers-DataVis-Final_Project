The Economic Impact of Higher Education Across Europe

A Data-Driven Visualization

By The Storytellers - Melica Omer Ali & Robert Tetteh Ashley

Regional Distribution of Educational Attainment

Understanding the geographic distribution of tertiary education is essential to identify patterns and disparities across European countries. This section explores how education levels vary regionally and how different nations have progressed in higher education attainment.

Percentage of People with Tertiary Education

The choropleth map illustrates the percentage of the population with tertiary education across Europe between the ages of 20-25, with darker shades representing higher percentages and lighter shades indicating lower levels. The map reveals significant regional disparities. Countries like Ireland , the United Kingdom, Croatia, and Serbia record percentages over 90% of the population. In contrast, nations such as Iceland, Turkey and Germany have lower levels, with less than 80% of their populations attaining higher education. Notably, countries in Central and Eastern Europe, such as Poland, Greece and the Czech Republic, have shown steady growth in tertiary education levels across the years reflecting recent educational reforms.

The Relationship Between Education and Income

Higher education is often linked to increased earnings and economic stability. This section examines how different levels of education impact income across various European countries, highlighting trends and disparities.

Average Income by Educational Level

The bar chart illustrates the relationship between educational attainment and income levels. It shows that as the education level increases, so does the average income. Individuals with a high school diploma earn the least, while those with a bachelor's degree see a noticeable jump in earnings. The highest incomes are observed among individuals with master's and doctorate degrees. This trend highlights the financial advantage of pursuing higher education. However, the chart also reveals any income gaps between different levels, emphasising the impact of education on earning potential. And in countries like Switzerland, Luxembourg, and Denmark, you notice the differences between the income of the level 0-2, 3-4 don't differ a lot from that of the level 5-8. In such a country, you realise education impacts your income.

Income Inequality by Education Level

This stacked bar chart illustrates the income distribution across different educational levels for a selection of European countries. On the Y-axis, countries are listed, while the X-axis represents income levels. Each bar is segmented by educational attainment levels, showing how much each educational group contributes to the total income distribution within a given country. The chart highlights how income inequality varies across education levels. For example, countries like Switzerland, Austria and Norway may show a smaller gap between educational levels, with high income levels across both secondary and tertiary education. On the other hand, countries like Luxembourg or Greece might show wider income disparities, with lower income for individuals with only primary or secondary education compared to those with tertiary education. By visualizing these income differences, the chart helps demonstrate the role education plays in shaping income inequality in different regions, allowing for easy comparisons between countries and the years.

Education, Income, and Employment Interactions

The alluvial chart maps the interaction between educational attainment, income, and employment across European countries. Generally, you notice that over the years, the level 3-4 education increases concerning the population and usually people with educational levels of 5-8 earn above the 30,000s. The data shows that in Ireland, Switzerland, Sweden, and Norway, individuals with tertiary education overwhelmingly secure high-income and stable employment with a high percentage. In contrast, in Serbia and North Macedonia, even those with university degrees face lower-income jobs, reflecting weaker labour market integration. In Poland, Romania, Bulgaria and Hungary, tertiary education leads to better job prospects but does not always guarantee high wages. Meanwhile, in Norway and Switzerland, education does not strongly correlate with income, with little wage progression from secondary to tertiary levels.

Education and Economic Performance

Educational attainment is often correlated with a nation's economic prosperity. This section explores the connection between education levels and GDP per capita to assess how education impacts economic performance.

Education, Employment, and Income by Country

This bubble chart visualizes the complex relationship between educational attainment, employment rates, and income by country. The size of each bubble represents GDP, offering a comprehensive view of how education impacts employment and income levels across different European nations. Switzerland and Norway have more than 85% of the population with tertiary education employed with higher incomes. Another thing to notice is although Germany has a bigger economy, the income from Switzerland, Luxembourg and Norway is much higher than that of Germany and this is usually due to the different standards of living across countries. An individual with an immediate level of education from Germany still earns about the same salary as an individual with an advanced level of education.

Educational Attainment vs. GDP per Capita in PPS

This scatter plot visualizes the relationship between the percentage of the population with tertiary education (higher education) and GDP per capita in Purchasing Power Standards (PPS) across European countries. PPS is a standardized measure that adjusts GDP to account for differences in living costs, with a value of 100 representing the EU average. Countries with higher percentages of tertiary-educated individuals generally appear above the 100 PPS baseline, indicating a correlation between higher education levels and stronger economic performance. For example, nations like Luxembourg, Norway, Switzerland and Ireland, with high tertiary education rates, tend to have GDP per capita significantly above the EU average, while countries with lower educational attainment, such as Serbia and Bosnia and Herzegovina, often show GDP per capita below the EU baseline. This visualization allows us to explore whether higher educational attainment is associated with higher national economic productivity, suggesting that a more educated workforce could contribute to economic growth and development.

Employment and Labor Market Trends

The labour market is significantly influenced by educational attainment. This section investigates employment rates, labour market participation, and unemployment patterns across different education levels.

Employment Rates by Education Level

This chart shows the changing trends in employment rates based on education level over time. It also shows the employment rate share of countries for the employment rate in Europe. It allows us to see how education impacts workforce participation, particularly for individuals with different levels of education. Each chart is a visualization for one education level.

Labor Market Participation vs. Education Level

This line chart compares labour market participation rates over time for individuals with different levels of education (primary, secondary, and tertiary). The x-axis represents the years, while the y-axis shows the employment rate for each education level. By examining the trends, we can observe how labour market participation for individuals with tertiary education has generally increased, reflecting the growing demand for higher-skilled workers. In contrast, participation rates for individuals with primary or secondary education may show slower growth or stagnation, highlighting the disparity in employment opportunities based on educational attainment.

Unemployment Rate by Education Level

The heatmap visualizes the unemployment rate across European countries, categorized by education level. Darker shades indicate higher unemployment rates, while lighter shades represent lower rates. A clear regional pattern emerges: countries in Southern Europe, such as Slovakia and Bosnia and Herzegovina, exhibit higher unemployment rates across the lower education levels, with particularly high rates among those with only primary or secondary education. In contrast, Northern European nations, such as Germany, Hungary, and Poland, show significantly lower unemployment, especially among individuals with tertiary education. Interestingly, in Slovakia, although there is high unemployment in the level 0-2 educational levels, there is a very low unemployment rate in the higher educational levels.

Workforce Participation by Educational Attainment

The chart compares countries such as Greece, Romania, Italy and France where tertiary education plays a significant role in workforce participation. In contrast, countries like Germany and Spain may show a higher percentage of workers with secondary or primary education. The pie chart provides insights into the educational composition of the labour market and helps illustrate the role of education in shaping workforce participation across different nations. You can interactively select a country and year to observe how workforce participation evolves and compare trends across nations.
